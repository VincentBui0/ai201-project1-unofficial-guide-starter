sauciestmeatball:

I'm currently attending for CS and quite enjoy it. They are very future-focused and embrace teaching their students about the way the world is evolving — like AI. I've seen AI curriculum incorporated even into business degrees at this school. They also do some cool work with NASA and are a strong research school if he is interested in that.

A big question is what are your son's future goals? Does he want to work within the NYC ecosystem one day? Does he plan to be involved with campus activities, attend events, network, and participate in co-ops?

Stevens' strong connections to NYC is a big pro of the university. But if you don't care about that, then it can be seen as a con. While in Hoboken, you are right across from NYC, and therefore will pay those high rental prices if he doesn't live on campus for all his time there. Obviously roommates help lower those costs, but be realistic that unless you're paying a lot, it'll be a very minimal apartment. If he does live on campus all 4 years, that's another hefty bill. Either way, you are paying a lot, but the tradeoff is getting lived experience in what many consider one of the greatest cities in the world. I think if he has the opportunity to spend even a few years there, it's a big life opportunity. But if you genuinely don't care about the NYC aspect, I do not think you'll find it's worth it compared to RIT or a state school.

RIT is also a larger campus with a more traditional feel. Stevens is smaller and tight-knit. Pros and cons of both depending on his goals.

Other things to consider: ROI. While Stevens is not ranked in the top 50 universities academically (though yes in the top 100), it is ranked as one of the top 10-15 universities on return on investment and career placement.

I cannot fully attest as I'm a very new student on a less traditional path, but for a smaller school, they seem to be very STEM focused. RIT sounds like a fantastic opportunity as well. Either way, your son will do great.

College name is only a piece of it. It's how well you do, how hard you work, and who you know. School name these days means less and less, so go with who makes the most sense financially, life-wise, and goal-wise. Student loan debt is no joke and interest rates are insane. You do not get a gold star for going into more debt unless you have a strong reason, and if you're not at an Ivy League, it's all more or less the same when HR looks at what school you attended.

If you can, take a trip to each university. Do a full campus walkthrough. Let them pitch to you, explore what the day looks like after classes are over. May cost a little up front but at least you'll have peace of mind you made the right choice. Best of luck.



Excellent-Tree-6716 (freshman, Mechanical Engineering):

I really do like Stevens as a school. You received a very generous aid package — paying $30K for tuition and dorm is fantastic compared to mine. Although it is only my first year, I think the engineering education Stevens offers is very good. It gives students an almost "into the deep end" type of education with the design classes.

There are some downsides. For an engineering school, it really only has two hands-on clubs: Stevens Electric Boat Works (which I am part of) and BAJA, the off-road racing club. The boat club is in my opinion amazing — the students are very friendly and you need zero prior knowledge to join. I have not joined BAJA but have heard from another student that it can be somewhat misogynistic and that they treat newcomers poorly if they come in without any background. Almost all other clubs on campus are social or leadership clubs.

I enjoy Stevens for how close it is to NYC since Hoboken is pretty lackluster in activities, and the PATH train is around $3 to go anywhere up to Penn Station. If your son likes humanities, this probably is not the best school for that as an engineering student — you really only have space for the mandatory English classes freshman year and 3 humanities courses over the other 3 years, unlike other colleges with more emphasis on being well-rounded. I cannot speak to social campus life since I commute daily.

Ok-Zucchini4816:

Stevens consistently delivers very strong early-career success for engineering and CS students, which is a core reason families choose it.

Around 97% of graduates achieve their desired outcome (employment or graduate school) within six months of graduation. Average early-career salary is well above national engineering averages.

CS majors specifically have reported career outcome rates around 95% with average starting salaries near $100,000 — excellent for the current job market.

The school ranks very well for mid-career salary potential, often placing Stevens in the top tier of value for STEM degrees nationally.

Students routinely participate in internships, co-ops, research, and industry projects during their degree, not just after. Employers value this in hiring.

Being minutes from New York City's job market, students can interview, intern, and work with companies like Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Deloitte, Lockheed Martin, and many others.

Career development includes personal advising, career fairs, resume support, and networking that is highly integrated with industry demand.

The environment is driven and professional, but you can find balance — scheduling, time management, and joining groups and social activities helps make the experience rewarding.
